# The Economy Takes Off: Tracking Flight Paths that Fuel Growth

**By Dr. Mahat Maalim, Data Scientist & Geospatial Analyst** • *November 11, 2025* • **8 min read**

For the 30DayMapChallenge in November 2025, Day 2 focuses on lines. I chose to visualize the departure destinations from Istanbul Airport (İstanbul Havalimanı), one of the world's largest aviation hubs. The maps above show flight connections radiating from Istanbul to destinations across Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Americas, illustrating how a single airport serves as a node in global connectivity.

## The Evolution of Travel Mode and Trade Activities

For most of human history, long-distance travel meant weeks or months of arduous journeys. Merchants transported goods by camel caravan across deserts, by ship across oceans, enduring storms and pirates. The Silk Road connected East and West over thousands of miles, but the journey took years. Letters between continents arrived months after being sent, if they arrived at all.

Railways in the 19th century began to change this, shrinking travel times from weeks to days within continents. Steamships made ocean crossings more reliable and faster. However, intercontinental travel remained a significant undertaking, requiring substantial time and resources. Only the wealthy or those with pressing business traveled internationally with any frequency.

Air travel emerged in the early 20th century but remained expensive and limited for decades. Early commercial flights made multiple stops, flights were uncomfortable, and only a small segment of society could afford tickets. The jet age beginning in the 1950s started to democratize international travel, though it wasn't until the 1980s and 1990s that air travel became accessible to middle-class populations in many countries.

Modern aviation has compressed global distances in ways previous generations could barely imagine. A journey that once took months now takes hours. This speed creates economic value that extends far past the airline industry itself. Businesses can establish operations across continents because executives can travel for meetings and return home the same week. Tourism has exploded because families can visit distant destinations during brief vacations. Fresh flowers grown in Kenya appear in European markets the next day. Time-sensitive manufacturing components move between factories on different continents.

## Flight Paths, Cargo Operations and Pandemic Adaptation

While passenger travel captures public attention, cargo transport has quietly become essential to how the modern economy functions. High-value, time-sensitive goods move by air including electronics, pharmaceuticals, fashion items, spare parts for machinery, perishable foods, and documents requiring rapid delivery. Before the pandemic, much of this cargo traveled in the holds of passenger aircraft, making efficient use of available space.

When COVID-19 arrived, it disrupted this system dramatically. Governments closed borders and people stopped traveling in early 2020, causing passenger flights to drop by up to 90% on many routes. Suddenly, cargo capacity vanished from the market just when demand for shipped goods was rising. Medical supplies, personal protective equipment, and later vaccines needed urgent transport. E-commerce packages piled up as consumers shifted to online shopping.

Airlines responded in different ways to this challenge. Some quickly converted passenger aircraft to carry cargo in the cabin, removing seats to maximize space. Others that had maintained or recently acquired dedicated freighter aircraft found themselves with a competitive advantage. Turkish Airlines, for example, continued cargo operations throughout the pandemic and actually expanded its cargo network while passenger operations were restricted. Emirates, Lufthansa, and others similarly pivoted to cargo.

Meanwhile, airlines that couldn't adapt faced severe difficulties. Those heavily dependent on passenger revenue with limited cargo capability watched income disappear while fixed costs remained. Several carriers that were already financially weak collapsed during 2020 and 2021. The pandemic showed that cargo wasn't just a supplementary revenue stream but could be the difference between survival and bankruptcy.

This experience changed how airlines and airports think about their operations. Cargo is now seen as a permanent priority rather than an afterthought. Many airports have expanded cargo facilities. Airlines have invested in freighter aircraft and cargo handling systems. The lesson was clear that economic resilience requires diversification across passenger and cargo operations.

## Strategic Value in Diplomacy and National Image

Aviation infrastructure serves purposes that extend into the realm of diplomacy and international relations. Governments invest heavily in airports and support national carriers partly for prestige and influence. A major international airport signals a country's participation in the global system. When a country lacks direct flight connections to major world cities, it remains more isolated, less visible, and less accessible for business hubs, technology centers, diplomatic, and dinner tables.

These direct flight routes facilitate relationships between countries in tangible ways. When an airline establishes service to a new destination, it creates channels for business partnerships, tourism flows, and cultural exchange. Governments often negotiate air service agreements as part of broader diplomatic relationships. Aviation access becomes intertwined with political connection, cultural exchange, and economic ties between nations.

Airports also shape international perceptions of countries in profound ways. The first impression many visitors have of a country comes from its airport. A modern, efficient facility suggests competence and development. Conversely, an outdated or poorly managed airport can reinforce negative stereotypes. This matters for attracting investment, tourism, and international events. Countries compete to host major conferences, sporting events, and business gatherings partly by showcasing their aviation infrastructure.

For airlines specifically, international routes carry symbolic weight that transcends commercial considerations. A national carrier flying to New York, London, Beijing, or Tokyo demonstrates that country's integration into the global economy. New routes often receive media coverage and government celebration. The aircraft itself, bearing the national flag carrier's livery, serves as a flying advertisement for the country. When Turkish Airlines expands to a new destination, it represents Turkey's growing connections and ambitions on the world stage.

## Turkey's Geographic Position and Istanbul Airport

Turkey sits at the intersection of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East. Throughout history, this location made it a crossroads for trade, migration, and cultural exchange. Constantinople controlled access between the Mediterranean and Black Seas. The Ottoman Empire leveraged this position to build a vast territory spanning three continents.

In the age of aviation, geography still matters fundamentally. An aircraft flying from Istanbul can reach a remarkable range of destinations within a few hours. Most of Europe, the Middle East, North Africa, and Central Asia lie within a five-hour flight radius. This makes Istanbul an efficient transfer point for passengers and cargo moving between these regions.

Having this geographic advantage in mind, Istanbul Airport was opened in 2018 to replace the erstwhile Atatürk Airport. When fully completed, it will have capacity for 200 million passengers annually, which makes it one of the world's largest airports. The facility includes extensive cargo handling areas. Turkish Airlines, the national carrier, has built a hub-and-spoke network centered on Istanbul, connecting passengers between destinations that lack direct flights.

The maps I created for this challenge illustrate this network visually. Lines extend from Istanbul across continents, reaching cities in Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Each line represents a flight route from Istanbul airport another destination airport. For the country, this brings employment in aviation and related services, tourism revenue, and the business activity that clusters around major transportation hubs.

The importance of this infrastructure became evident during the pandemic when global travel collapsed. While many airports saw traffic nearly disappear, Istanbul maintained operations as a transfer point for cargo and limited passenger flows. Turkish Airlines continued flying to destinations others had abandoned, maintaining connections that proved valuable when travel began recovering. This resilience demonstrated the strategic value of having built robust aviation infrastructure before the crisis hit. As the adage goes, "prepare for the worst and hope for the best".

For Turkey, Istanbul Airport represents both an economic investment and a statement about the country's role in connecting continents and civilizations in the 21st century.
