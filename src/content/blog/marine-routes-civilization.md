# Maritime Routes and the Foundations of Civilization

**By Dr. Mahat Maalim, Data Scientist & Geospatial Analyst** • *November 10, 2024* • **6 min read**

Throughout history, the sea has been humanity's most enduring pathway of connection. Long before the age of highways and air travel, maritime routes carried the movement of goods, people, and ideas across continents, forming the backbone of global trade and cultural exchange. Empires flourished along these routes, cities rose where ships anchored, and civilizations were shaped by the tides that brought both opportunity and influence. The sea remains not just a geographical feature but a force that has guided the course of human progress, linking distant shores in a shared story of exploration and exchange.

Lighthouses emerged as silent guardians of these routes, standing at the edges of continents to guide sailors through fog, darkness, and storms. Their light symbolizes safety, direction, and resilience, reminding us that every voyage, whether economic or cultural, relies on guidance and trust in the unknown.

## The Maritime Geography of Italy, Türkiye, and Somalia

Italy sits at the heart of the Mediterranean Sea, a region that has served as a crossroads of trade for more than two millennia. Its coastline connects Europe to North Africa and the Near East, and its ports such as Genoa, Venice, and Naples once dominated maritime commerce and naval exploration. According to OpenStreetMap, Italy has **1,236 active and historical lighthouses**, a reflection of its long maritime tradition and its dependence on the sea for trade, transport, and cultural contact.

Türkiye occupies an equally strategic position, bridging Europe and Asia through the Bosphorus and Dardanelles straits. These narrow waterways have been among the most important maritime corridors in world history, controlling the passage between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean. Türkiye hosts **701 lighthouses** scattered across the Aegean, Marmara, and Black Sea coasts. They mark centuries of navigation between empires, from Byzantium to the Ottoman period, and now continue to serve one of the busiest sea passages on Earth.

Somalia, by contrast, lies along the Horn of Africa with the longest coastline on the African continent, measuring **3,333 kilometers**. It connects Africa to Asia and the Arabian Peninsula and sits at the entrance to the Red Sea, close to some of the world's key shipping lanes. Despite this remarkable position, Somalia's maritime economy has not yet reached its full potential.

According to OpenStreetMap, the country has only **18 identified lighthouses**, a small number for such a vast coastal area. Yet this same coastline holds immense promise for fisheries, shipping, and regional trade, forming a natural bridge between continents that once shaped ancient maritime routes across the Indian Ocean.

## Mapping the Lighthouses

On the first day of the 30DayMapChallenge, I created a Lighthouse Map for Italy, Türkiye, and Somalia using Python and data from OpenStreetMap—to visualize their coastal beacons and highlight how geography continues to influence economic and cultural development. The map reminds us that while technology and trade have evolved, the principles of connection, safety, and guidance, once symbolized by lighthouses, remain central to how societies thrive along the world's seas.
